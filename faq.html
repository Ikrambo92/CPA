Frequently Asked Questions

What are business rates?
Business rates, also known as non-domestic rates, are taxes levied on non-domestic properties in the United Kingdom. They are a significant source of income for local authorities and contribute to funding local services.
How are business rates calculated?
Business rates are calculated based on the rateable value of a property and the multiplier set by the government. The rateable value is an estimate of the property's open market rental value on a specific date. It is reassessed periodically by the Valuation Office Agency.
Am I eligible for any business rates relief or exemptions?
Eligibility for business rates relief or exemptions depends on factors such as the property's rateable value, business type, location, and the specific relief scheme. Different relief schemes have different criteria, so it is advisable to consult with the relevant authorities or a business rates specialist.
What types of properties are exempt from business rates?
Certain properties may be exempt from business rates, such as agricultural land and buildings, religious buildings, and some properties used for charitable purposes. However, exemptions can vary, and it is essential to check with the local council or relevant authorities for specific details.
Are there any specific reliefs for small businesses?
Yes, the Small Business Rate Relief (SBRR) scheme provides relief to small businesses meeting certain criteria. It offers reduced or zero rates for eligible businesses with a low rateable value. The exact relief and eligibility criteria can vary among different regions.
Can I appeal my business rates if I think they are incorrect?
Yes, if you believe your business rates are incorrect, you have the right to appeal. You can contact the Valuation Office Agency to initiate the appeals process and provide evidence to support your case. It is recommended to seek professional advice when considering a rates appeal.
Are there any specific rates or reliefs for certain industries or sectors?
Yes, certain industries or sectors may be eligible for specific rates or reliefs. For example, there may be targeted relief schemes for the hospitality sector or business rates discounts for newly developed properties. It is advisable to check with the local council or authorities for industry-specific schemes.
What happens if I don't pay my business rates?
If you fail to pay your business rates, the local council has the authority to take enforcement action, such as issuing a summons, applying for a liability order, or using bailiffs to collect the debt. It's important to communicate with your council if you're facing difficulties in paying your rates to explore possible payment arrangements.
